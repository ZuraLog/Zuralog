# Nutrition - Expert Reference

## When to use this skill
Use for questions about macronutrients, calorie targets, meal timing, protein intake, and dietary strategies for health and fitness goals.

## Core principles

- **Calories in versus calories out determines body weight over time.** Every dietary approach — keto, intermittent fasting, plant-based, carnivore — works through this single mechanism. No food is inherently fattening; context, quantity, and total energy balance decide the outcome.
- **Protein is the non-negotiable macro.** It is the only macro that builds and preserves muscle tissue. It also has the highest thermic effect (20–30% of calories consumed are spent digesting it) and the strongest satiety signal. Protein should be set first before any other macro is calculated.
- **Dietary fat has a minimum floor, not just a preference.** Fat below 0.3g per lb of bodyweight suppresses testosterone, oestrogen, and other hormones that regulate mood, recovery, and metabolic function. This is a hard floor, not a guideline.
- **Fibre regulates hunger, blood sugar, and gut health.** A target of 25–35g per day from whole food sources reduces energy spikes that trigger overeating and supports the microbiome. Fibre also adds volume to meals, which helps manage hunger during a caloric deficit.
- **Hydration is a performance and appetite variable.** Mild dehydration is frequently misread as hunger. Meeting water targets improves energy, focus, and training output. Start with 0.5–1oz per lb of bodyweight per day and adjust up for sweat output and climate.
- **Meal timing is secondary to total daily intake.** Pre- and post-workout nutrition matters at the margins — for well-trained athletes chasing the last 5% of performance. For the vast majority of people, hitting daily protein and calorie targets consistently matters far more than the window in which they do it.
- **Adherence beats perfection.** The best dietary plan is the one the person can follow for months and years. A modest deficit maintained consistently outperforms an aggressive deficit abandoned after three weeks.

## Common scenarios and how to handle them

**Calculating maintenance calories:** Use bodyweight in lbs multiplied by an activity multiplier — sedentary (×14), lightly active (×15–16), moderately active (×17–18), very active (×19–20). Treat the first two weeks as calibration. If weight drifts, adjust by 100–150 calories and re-evaluate.
Treat this as a starting estimate — calibrate by tracking actual weight change over 2 weeks and adjusting by 100–150 cal/day as needed; users carrying excess body weight should start at the lower end of their activity multiplier.

**Setting up a caloric deficit or surplus:** A deficit of 300–500 calories per day produces 0.5–1 lb of weekly loss — the range that preserves muscle and hormone function. For lean muscle gain, 200–300 calories above maintenance limits fat accumulation. Beginners can recomp at maintenance and do not need a surplus immediately.

**Hitting protein targets on a plant-based diet:** Plant proteins are often incomplete, but combining varied sources across the day (legumes, grains, tofu, tempeh, seitan, edamame) fills the gaps without precise meal-by-meal matching. Aim for the upper end of the range (1g/lb) to account for slightly lower digestibility. Pea, rice, or soy protein powder closes remaining gaps.

**Pre- and post-workout nutrition:** A meal with 20–40g of protein and moderate carbohydrates within 1–2 hours before and after training supports performance and recovery. The "anabolic window" extends several hours post-session — not 30 minutes. Hitting daily protein targets matters far more than precise timing.

**Managing hunger during a caloric deficit:** Prioritise high-volume, high-fibre, high-protein foods — vegetables, lean proteins, legumes, broth-based soups. Distribute protein across 3–5 meals of 20–40g each; this outperforms front- or back-loading for satiety. Poor sleep raises appetite by 300–400 calories the following day — address sleep before adding more food.

## Numbers and thresholds that matter

| Variable | Target |
|---|---|
| Protein intake | 0.7–1g per lb of bodyweight per day |
| Minimum dietary fat | 0.3g per lb of bodyweight per day |
| Caloric deficit (fat loss) | 300–500 calories/day below maintenance |
| Caloric surplus (lean bulk) | 200–300 calories/day above maintenance |
| Sustainable fat loss rate | 0.5–1 lb per week |
| Fibre target | 25–35g per day |
| Water intake | 0.5–1oz per lb of bodyweight per day |
| Protein per meal for muscle protein synthesis | 20–40g |

## What to avoid saying

- **Never recommend below 1,200 calories per day without medical supervision.** Below this threshold, meeting micronutrient needs becomes nearly impossible, metabolic adaptation accelerates, and the risk of nutritional deficiency rises sharply. If a person's maintenance is low enough that a 500-calorie deficit approaches 1,200, tighten the deficit or increase activity instead.
- **Never demonise entire food groups.** Carbohydrates do not cause fat gain in isolation. Fat does not clog arteries in isolation. Processed food is not toxic in moderation. Moralising food creates disordered eating patterns and shame cycles that undermine long-term adherence. Use quantity and context, not fear.
- **Never promise specific timeline results.** "Lose 20 lbs in 30 days" and similar claims ignore individual variation in metabolism, adherence, water retention, and starting body composition. Provide rate-of-change ranges and frame them as estimates that require ongoing adjustment, not guarantees.
