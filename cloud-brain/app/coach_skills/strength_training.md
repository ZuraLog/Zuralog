# Strength Training - Expert Reference

## When to use this skill
Use for questions about resistance training, hypertrophy, rep schemes, strength programming, and periodisation.

## Core principles

- **Progressive overload is the only mechanism for long-term strength and size gains.** The body adapts to a given stress and stops responding; you must continuously increase that stress — through added weight, more reps, extra sets, or reduced rest — or progress stalls.
- **Compound movements first, accessories second.** Squat, deadlift, bench press, overhead press, and row deliver the highest stimulus per unit of time. Isolation work fills gaps; it does not replace compounds.
- **Rep ranges are a dial, not a rule.** Strength peaks in the 1–5 rep range with loads above 85% of one-rep max. Hypertrophy responds best to 6–12 reps at 67–85% one-rep max. Muscular endurance extends from 12 reps upward. Overlap exists — you can build muscle at 5 reps and strength at 12 — but the ranges optimise specific adaptations.
- **Rest periods match the goal.** Strength work demands 2–5 minutes between sets so the nervous system recovers fully. Hypertrophy work sits at 60–90 seconds, keeping metabolic stress elevated. Cutting rest below these thresholds for the wrong goal reduces output and blunts adaptation.
- **Each muscle group needs a minimum of 2 direct training sessions per week.** Once per week provides a maintenance stimulus but not an optimal growth signal. Three or more sessions per week can work for advanced lifters if volume per session is managed.
- **Deload weeks are maintenance, not laziness.** Accumulated fatigue masks fitness. Reducing volume by 40–50% every 4–6 weeks allows the nervous system and connective tissue to recover, and the lifter typically returns stronger.
- **Technique gates load.** Increasing weight on a broken pattern accelerates injury risk and trains the wrong motor patterns. Fix form first, then add load.

## Common scenarios and how to handle them

**Beginner choosing a program:** Beginners respond to almost any consistent stimulus, so complexity is the enemy. A full-body 3-day programme (e.g. 3×5 or 5×5 structure) hitting squat, hinge, push, pull, and carry each session builds the neural efficiency and technique base required for everything that follows. Do not start an upper/lower or body-part split until 6–12 months of consistent training have established a movement foundation.

**Plateau after months of progress:** First audit sleep and nutrition — an under-recovered, under-fed athlete cannot adapt regardless of programming. If recovery is adequate, introduce a planned variation: change the rep range by 2–3 reps, add a pause or tempo, or swap the primary lift for a close variant (e.g. Romanian deadlift instead of conventional). If the plateau persists beyond 3–4 weeks, reduce volume by 30% for one week, then reload.

**Hypertrophy focus vs. strength focus:** Both require progressive overload. The main difference is working rep range and rest periods. For hypertrophy, accumulate 10–20 sets per muscle group per week across 2–3 sessions, work in the 6–12 rep range, and keep rest at 60–90 seconds. For strength, keep weekly sets lower (5–10 per primary pattern), prioritise 1–5 rep work, and take full 2–5 minute rest periods. A concurrent block training approach can serve both goals at intermediate level, but specialise when progress demands it.

**Training around a minor injury:** First distinguish between sharp, joint-located pain (stop immediately, seek evaluation) and dull muscular soreness or minor strains (manageable with modification). For the latter, replace the aggravating movement with a pain-free variant — front squat for knee pain on back squat, dumbbell press for shoulder impingement on barbell bench — and maintain volume on unaffected patterns. Maintain cardiovascular work throughout.

**Programming for fat loss while preserving muscle:** Maintain training intensity and movement quality; reduce total volume by 10–20% to account for reduced recovery capacity from a caloric deficit. Protein must remain at or above 0.7–1g per lb of bodyweight. Prioritise compound lifts. Cardio is additive, not a replacement for the training stimulus that signals the body to retain muscle.

## Numbers and thresholds that matter

| Variable | Target |
|---|---|
| Strength rep range | 1–5 reps, ≥85% 1RM |
| Hypertrophy rep range | 6–12 reps, 67–85% 1RM |
| Endurance rep range | 12+ reps, <67% 1RM |
| Rest — strength sets | 2–5 minutes |
| Rest — hypertrophy sets | 60–90 seconds |
| Minimum training frequency per muscle | 2× per week |
| Progressive overload target | 2.5–5% load increase per week (beginners); 1–2.5% per month (intermediate) |
| Deload frequency | Every 4–6 weeks |
| Weekly sets per muscle for hypertrophy | 10–20 sets (across all sessions) |

## What to avoid saying

- **Never say "just lift heavier" without context.** Load progression requires adequate recovery, sufficient technique, and a clear plan. Telling someone to add weight without assessing their current status is not coaching — it is noise.
- **Never recommend 1RM testing for beginners.** A true one-rep max attempt on an athlete who lacks movement competence and neural efficiency produces unreliable numbers and meaningful injury risk. Use a 3–5RM and an estimated max formula instead.
- **Never suggest training through sharp pain.** Dull delayed-onset muscle soreness is expected. Sharp, acute, or joint-specific pain is a warning signal that must not be overridden. The advice to "push through it" has ended training careers.
