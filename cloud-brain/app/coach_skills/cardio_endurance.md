# Cardio & Endurance - Expert Reference

## When to use this skill
Use for questions about cardiovascular training, heart rate zones, VO2 max improvement, running programs, and endurance programming.

## Core principles

- **Aerobic base is the foundation of all endurance performance.** The aerobic system powers sustained effort, accelerates recovery between hard intervals, and protects the athlete from overuse injury. Skipping base-building in favour of intensity produces short-term gains and long-term stagnation or breakdown.
- **The 80/20 rule governs elite and amateur endurance athletes alike.** Approximately 80% of weekly training volume should be performed at easy, aerobic effort (zone 1–2) and only 20% at moderate-to-hard intensity. Most self-coached athletes invert this ratio and spend too much time in the "junk zone" — too hard to recover from, too easy to drive adaptation.
- **Heart rate zones are the primary tool for controlling training stimulus.** Training by feel alone is unreliable, especially in heat, fatigue, or stress. Zones provide a repeatable, objective anchor. Calibrate zones to the individual — not to population averages printed on gym walls.
- **Consistency beats intensity for long-term cardiovascular adaptation.** Showing up 4–5 days per week at easy effort for 6 months builds more aerobic capacity than sporadic hard sessions. The cardiovascular system adapts slowly; it rewards regular, cumulative stress over weeks and months.
- **Volume increases before intensity.** When building a program, accumulate weekly distance or time at easy effort for 4–8 weeks before adding structured interval work. Adding intensity to an inadequate aerobic base raises injury risk and limits the quality of hard sessions.
- **Hard sessions require full recovery between them.** Most athletes can sustain 2 high-intensity sessions per week without accumulating excessive fatigue. A third hard session per week is viable only for experienced athletes with a large aerobic base and optimal recovery.
- **Recovery days are training days.** Easy activity or full rest lowers cortisol, restores glycogen, and allows structural adaptation. Removing rest to add more volume reduces the quality of all subsequent sessions.

## Common scenarios and how to handle them

**Building an aerobic base from scratch:** Start with 3–4 sessions per week of 20–40 minutes at fully conversational pace — full sentences without pausing to breathe, heart rate in zone 2 (60–70% max HR). Increase total weekly time by no more than 10% per week. After 6–8 weeks, aerobic efficiency improves enough to introduce one structured session per week.

**Improving a 5K or 10K time:** A plateau almost always signals an aerobic base too small to support faster racing. Increase easy volume for 4–6 weeks first. Then add one tempo session per week (zone 3–4, 20–40 minutes total) and eventually one interval session (e.g. 6×800m at goal pace with equal rest). Keep 80% of all other running easy.

**Explaining zone 2 training:** Zone 2 is the effort where the body burns primarily fat, the lungs are comfortable, and the athlete can hold a conversation without pausing — approximately 60–70% of maximum heart rate. Sustained zone 2 work over weeks increases mitochondrial density and fat oxidation, so easy runs gradually become faster at the same heart rate.

**Combining cardio with strength training:** The interference effect (cardio blunting strength gains) is real but manageable. Separate sessions by at least 6 hours, or do the priority session first. Avoid hard cardio on the same day as heavy lower-body lifting. Easy zone 1–2 cardio on strength recovery days is beneficial, not harmful.

**Returning after a break:** Cardiovascular capacity drops 5–10% after 2–4 weeks off and significantly more after 2–3 months. Return at 50–60% of previous volume and intensity for 2 weeks regardless of how the body feels — connective tissue lags behind cardiovascular recovery and is easily overwhelmed. Apply the 10% weekly increase rule strictly from re-entry.

## Numbers and thresholds that matter

| Variable | Target |
|---|---|
| Zone 2 heart rate | 60–70% of maximum heart rate |
| Maximum HR estimate (rough guide) | 220 minus age in years |
| Weekly volume increase limit | 10% per week |
| Hard sessions per week (sustainable) | 2 sessions (3 for experienced athletes only) |
| VO2 max improvement timeline | 8–12 weeks of consistent aerobic training |
| Easy-to-hard training ratio (80/20 rule) | 80% easy (zone 1–2), 20% moderate-to-hard (zone 3–5) |
| Easy pace description | Conversational — full sentences, no pausing to breathe |
| Base-building phase before adding intervals | 4–8 weeks |

## What to avoid saying

- **Never recommend all-out sprints or maximum-effort sessions for beginners.** An untrained cardiovascular system cannot buffer the metabolic stress of high-intensity interval training safely. Beginners need aerobic base work first. Recommending HIIT as a starting point increases injury risk, drives rapid burnout, and undermines adherence.
- **Never suggest skipping rest days when training volume is high.** More-is-better thinking is the single most common cause of overuse injury in endurance athletes. Rest days are when adaptation happens. Removing them reduces training quality across every subsequent session and raises the risk of stress fractures, tendinopathy, and overtraining syndrome.
- **Never give generic "just run more" advice.** Volume without structure causes injury in most cases. Every recommendation should address pace (which zone), frequency (how many days), and the 10% rule. Running more at the wrong intensity in the wrong volume increment is worse than running less — it accumulates fatigue without driving useful adaptation.
